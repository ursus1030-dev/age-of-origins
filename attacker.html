Attacks

How to attack, when to attack, and how to read enemy reports before you commit.

← Back to Combat

Solo attacks

Hitting a target alone is the fastest way to kill enemy troops — and the fastest way to lose your own if you walk into a trap. Two habits keep you safe: scout before you send, and never touch an online player without speedups ready.

Your solo march

Start from the standard group march — one soldier of each type, plus as many snipers and long-range cannons as you can field. For a solo hit, add to that:

  • 2,000–5,000 shredders, scaled to how strong the enemy is.
  • As many zombies as you can bring.
  • Lasers, depending on their level.

Timing

If your goal is to kill troops, attack when all of theirs are home — an empty city gives you nothing. Online targets are always riskier, so observe first and wait for the right moment instead of sending on impulse.

Watch out: against an online player, always keep speed ready. Without it you can't pull back from a trap in time.

SOS cancels your troop losses in a fight — but only when you fight completely alone, and only once a day. It does nothing in a group.

Rally attacks

A rally is the whole alliance hitting one target together. There are two ways to coordinate, depending on whether the target is awake.

SA — synchronized attack

Used for precision, often against online targets. The lead picks a time from the 'Time Gift' countdown in the top-right corner, posts it in Alliance Chat, and at that exact second everyone presses send and speeds in. No hesitation — if you trust the team, you send.

TA — timed rally

The classic rally on an offline target. Each player calls out their march time; the one farthest away starts, and everyone else lines up to land at the same moment.

Check before you commit

Before the rally lands, make sure the target hasn't reinforced, recalled, or bubbled — all visible in Alliance Battles. If the target is in fog, you can't see their reinforcements directly: launch a probe rally, open Alliance Battles, and count the defenders. It should be one (or zero for a flag). Then cancel the rally.

If a bubble goes up mid-rally, everyone recalls. Landing on a bubble wastes the march and can set up a counter-trap.

Reading battle reports

Half of attacking well is reading what's in front of you — before you send, and after the dust settles.

Real attack or fake?

Look at the fleet on the field. A real attack brings a titan or a plane. If you see neither, it's a fake attack meant to bait a reaction — don't overcommit your reinforcements to it.

How group battles count

When several cities hit one target together, the report isn't a simple sum. A few rules from testing:

  • Troop stats are averaged across the group, weighted by how many troops of each type are present.
  • Troop tiers are not averaged — a higher tier keeps its own kill-and-loss advantage.
  • A weak city in the group can drag down a strong city's individual result.
  • Titan stats stack instead of averaging, and titan kills are split by each player's titan strength — while troop kills come out equal for both sides.

Takeaway: in a group hit, what you bring that can't be averaged away — tier and titan strength — is what moves the result.
